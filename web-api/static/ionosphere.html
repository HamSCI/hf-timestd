Solar Diurnal Cycle — One Path at a Time

Select a station to see how its signal strengths vary with solar illumination. The gold curve shows solar elevation at the path midpoint. Low frequencies (2.5, 3.3, 5 MHz) are absorbed by the D-layer during daytime — SNR drops when the sun is up. High frequencies (15, 20, 25 MHz) propagate only when the F-layer is ionised — SNR rises with the sun. The crossover frequency depends on path geometry and season.

Frequency Dispersion — Experimental Group-Delay TEC

D_clock(f) = D_common + 1.344 × TEC / f². Plotting D_clock vs 1/f² for multiple frequencies with the same propagation mode, the slope gives slant TEC along the oblique HF ray path. The fit is filtered to same-mode points only — mixing 1F2 and 2F2 hops would conflate geometry differences with dispersion.

Limitations: Group-delay TEC is a noise-dominated validation product. D_clock uncertainty (~1–2 ms) is comparable to the TEC-induced dispersion between adjacent frequencies. R² < 0.7 or RMS > 2 ms indicates the estimate is unreliable. Negative TEC is unphysical (mode misassignment or multipath). The primary ionospheric TEC product is carrier-phase dTEC on the dTEC page. Typical slant TEC: 5–20 TECU night, 20–80 TECU day. Compare with SWPC US-TEC.

Events & Space Weather

Solar flares cause Sudden Ionospheric Disturbances (SIDs) — increased D-layer absorption drops SNR on lower frequencies within minutes. Geomagnetic storms (high Kp) disrupt F-layer propagation. Mode transitions mark ionospheric state changes.
